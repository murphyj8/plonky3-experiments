SUGGESTED SALES DEMO
The hardest part of selling STARKs to non-technical buyers is the succinctness property: that a prover can do an enormous amount of work and the verifier can check it in a tiny fraction of that time, and that the verifier doesn't need to trust the prover. Concretely:

The prover does the work; the verifier just checks the receipt — and the receipt is unforgeable.

The cleanest demo I can imagine, building directly on SumAir, is a two-pane web app or CLI with a stopwatch:

Concept: "Prove the counter"
A single web page (or two side-by-side terminals) labelled PROVER and AUDITOR:

Setup (sales person on stage):

Slider: "How many steps? n = 2^k where k from 3 to 20"
"Starting number: init" input
The prover shows a live counter ticking up n times, then says: "I claim acc_final = init + (n-1)".
Prove button (PROVER pane):

On click, runs generate_sum_trace(init, n) + prove_stark(...).
Shows two clocks:
Time spent computing the answer (the trace)
Time spent producing the proof (the prover)
Shows proof size in KB.
Outputs a small text "receipt" the auditor can copy.
Verify button (AUDITOR pane):

Auditor pastes the receipt + the claimed acc_final value.
On click, runs verify_stark(...).
Shows the verify clock — typically tens of milliseconds, almost flat regardless of n.
Big green ✓ "VERIFIED" or red ✗ "REJECTED".
The "aha" moment — three live demonstrations for the audience:

Scale: bump the slider from n = 2^10 to n = 2^20. Prove time multiplies by ~1000×. Verify time stays nearly constant. Sales pitch: "the prover scales with the work; the verifier doesn't."
Tampering: auditor edits one digit of acc_final in the receipt before clicking Verify. Instantly rejected. Sales pitch: "the receipt is mathematically tamper-evident."
No re-execution: emphasise that the auditor never had to re-run the loop. The verifier checks the receipt, not the work. Sales pitch: "your customer can audit a million-step calculation without doing a million steps."
Why SumAir (not FibonacciAir) is the right vehicle
Counting up by 1 is something everyone can picture: "I claim I added 1 to a counter a million times".
The "expected output" is a single number any audience member can compute on their phone for small n, then for large n they have to trust the proof — which is exactly the moment you want them to feel.
The AIR has only one main column, two public inputs, and one transition constraint, so it doesn't bury the message in mathematical detail.
Stretch (one slide, no code)
After the live demo, one diagram showing where this fits:

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Trusted: math (Goldilocks field, FRI, Merkle trees)    │
│  Untrusted: the party doing the work (the prover)       │
│  Cheap: the party checking the work (the verifier)      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
Pair with one-line industry hooks the sales team can plug into their own scripts, e.g.:

Finance: "audit a year of trades without re-running the books"
Supply chain: "verify a chain of custody without seeing every leg"
AI: "prove a model produced this output, without revealing the weights"
The implementation cost for this demo is low: a small Axum/Actix web server wrapping make_stark_config + prove_stark + verify_stark, with a one-page React or HTML/JS frontend. The Rust side already exists; the demo is mostly UX glue. Realistic effort: half a day to a day.
